Reflective Thinking:

Summary: Week 1 introduced the evolution of cloud computing, from early virtualisation technologies to modern multi-cloud ecosystems. It covered the three core service models — IaaS, PaaS, SaaS — and examined deployment models such as public, private, hybrid, and community cloud. Comparisons between AWS, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure highlighted service portfolios, scalability options, and pricing structures.

In my current role, we use AWS S3 buckets for data storage and analytics workloads, while Azure Active Directory underpins enterprise identity management. My cross-platform expertise, supported by certifications such as Microsoft 365 Fundamentals and Microsoft Information Protection and Compliance Administrator Associate, enables me to navigate both ecosystems effectively and ensure security, governance, and compliance alignment. A critical takeaway this week was recognising that while multi-cloud adoption offers resilience and vendor flexibility, it can also create governance challenges and increase the demand for skilled personnel capable of managing multiple platforms.

Application: This understanding directly informs workload placement and procurement decisions, allowing me to balance performance, compliance, and cost efficiency when advising on enterprise cloud strategy.

Forward Planning: I intend to explore further vendor management tools such as AWS Organizations and Azure Lighthouse to strengthen cross-platform governance and streamline policy enforcement across multi-cloud environments.

Activity:

Coursework: Formative Discussion: ROCCA and TOGAF

Reflective Thinking:

Summary: This week focused on frameworks that guide cloud architecture. TOGAF offered a structured method for aligning IT with business strategy, the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) reinforced disciplined processes in cloud solution delivery, and Infrastructure as Code (IaC) highlighted how automation improves consistency, scalability, and compliance. Collectively, these approaches help ensure cloud deployments are strategic, controlled, and repeatable.

Reflection: TOGAF is particularly relevant because it encourages a business-first approach to cloud architecture. In practice, I have often seen technical teams prioritise tool implementation without aligning outcomes to business needs. TOGAF's phased Architecture Development Method provides a reminder that strategy and governance should guide deployment choices. SDLC similarly resonated, as cloud environments still require traditional structure for planning, testing, and stakeholder engagement. IaC struck a strong chord with me; having experienced the operational inefficiencies of manual deployments, I see how IaC reduces configuration drift and enforces auditability, which is vital in regulated industries.

Application: Applying TOGAF in my environment could help ensure our AWS and Azure architectures remain strategically aligned and compliant. Embedding SDLC principles into cloud change management would improve testing and rollback, reducing production risk. IaC is already present in Azure deployments for compliance monitoring, but scaling it across both AWS and Azure would improve efficiency while strengthening adherence to frameworks such as GDPR and FCA guidelines.

Forward Planning: I intend to explore how TOGAF ADM can be tailored to hybrid and multi-cloud strategies, and how policy-as-code can complement IaC to improve governance. Embedding SDLC checkpoints into IaC pipelines will also be a priority, ensuring automation remains aligned with compliance and testing requirements.

Summary: This week, we examined hybrid cloud strategies, which integrate on-premises infrastructure with public or private cloud services. We explored patterns such as cloud bursting for peak demand, maintaining data sovereignty for compliance, and segmenting workloads according to security and performance requirements.

Reflection: While I have managed both cloud and on-premises environments in the past, studying structured hybrid models provided a deeper understanding of integration strategies. Our AWS–Azure split not only optimises performance but also mitigates single-vendor dependency and maintains compliance with frameworks such as GDPR and PCI DSS.

Forward Planning: I intend to explore AWS Outposts and Azure Arc to extend hybrid capabilities, enabling unified policy management and more seamless compliance enforcement across both platforms.

Reflective Thinking:

Summary: This week explored cloud-native technologies, focusing on the Twelve-Factor App methodology (Wiggins, 2017) and Kubernetes as a container orchestration tool. The content emphasised how cloud-native design principles ensure scalability, resilience, and agility in application development. The lecturecast on AI-driven cloud computing further demonstrated how automation and intelligent orchestration are transforming deployment and management of containerised workloads.

Reflection: The 12-Factor methodology resonated with me as it provides a practical checklist for building applications that are portable and cloud-ready. In practice, I have observed how ignoring principles such as strict separation of config from code can create fragility when scaling services. Kubernetes was equally significant, as it operationalises these principles through automated scaling, load balancing, and self-healing. Academic insights reinforce these ideas: Hamzaoui et al. (2024) highlight that containers deliver lightweight, portable environments that outperform traditional hypervisors, while Bonomi et al. (2012) and Gupta et al. (2016) illustrate how fog and edge computing extend cloud-native benefits closer to users, reducing latency for IoT and real-time applications. The integration of AI into orchestration workflows also sparked reflection on how predictive scaling and policy enforcement could further reduce operational overheads.

Application: In financial services, these principles directly apply to scaling transaction-processing systems and analytics platforms. For example, containerising microservices that handle compliance checks could improve resilience while Kubernetes ensures they are dynamically scaled based on demand. AI-driven orchestration adds further value by anticipating workload spikes, which is critical for trading platforms where milliseconds matter.

Forward Planning: I plan to experiment with Kubernetes deployments that integrate policy-as-code to enforce compliance while leveraging AI models for predictive scaling. Additionally, I aim to apply 12-Factor principles when reviewing internal applications, ensuring portability and resilience remain embedded in future cloud-native solutions.

Development Team Project: Cloud Infrastructure and Framework Report

Summary:

This week focused on AI-driven cloud computing and cloud design tools. The lecturecast explored how AI enhances automation, security, and cost optimisation across cloud environments. We examined practical applications such as dynamic resource allocation, predictive fault detection, and compliance monitoring. The seminar also compared scripting approaches (Bash, Python, Terraform) with GUI-based tools (Azure Portal, AWS Console), weighing flexibility against accessibility. Finally, we discussed how AI-driven automation is reshaping administrator roles, shifting emphasis towards governance, strategy, and architecture (Bonomi et al., 2012; Hamzaoui et al., 2024).

Reflection:

I found AI’s role in proactive fault detection particularly valuable, as predictive analytics can prevent downtime in financial services where resilience is critical (Gupta et al., 2016). Similarly, AI-driven security tools such as Microsoft Sentinel strengthen incident response and support compliance with ISO 27001. Reflecting on scripting versus GUI tools, I realised that while GUIs remain useful for troubleshooting, scripting and Infrastructure-as-Code pipelines are indispensable for standardisation at scale. The shift in administrator responsibilities stood out: rather than reducing demand, AI expands the skill set required, emphasising governance, AIOps, and cross-team collaboration (NIST, 2018).

Application:

In my current role, AI-based cost optimisation tools like AWS Compute Optimizer could directly reduce cloud expenditure, while self-healing Kubernetes clusters would strengthen service continuity. Continuous compliance monitoring would support regulatory obligations such as FCA and GDPR. By combining scripting for automation with GUI tools for ad-hoc use, our team can balance efficiency with accessibility for less technical colleagues.

Forward Planning:

I plan to develop stronger scripting expertise in Python and Terraform to extend automation and integrate CI/CD pipelines. I also aim to experiment with AIOps platforms that embed AI into observability and compliance monitoring, ensuring greater resilience and governance in multi-cloud deployments. Building these skills will prepare me for hybrid roles that combine technical architecture, automation, and AI-driven governance.
