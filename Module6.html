IaC is already present in Azure deployments for compliance monitoring, but scaling it across both AWS and Azure would improve efficiency while strengthening adherence to frameworks such as GDPR and FCA guidelines.

Forward Planning: I intend to explore how TOGAF ADM can be tailored to hybrid and multi-cloud strategies, and how policy-as-code can complement IaC to improve governance. Embedding SDLC checkpoints into IaC pipelines will also be a priority, ensuring automation remains aligned with compliance and testing requirements.

Summary:

This week focused on cloud security and compliance, particularly how cloud controls map to frameworks such as ISO 27001, NIST SP 800-53, PCI DSS, and GDPR. The lecturecast explored cloud-native technologies and containerisation, while the coursework involved performing a security audit of an OpenStack deployment using OpenVAS. This highlighted risks such as unpatched services and weak authentication, and how they align with industry standards (ISO, 2022; NIST, 2020; PCI SSC, 2022; European Union, 2016).

Reflection:

The OpenVAS audit made me reflect on how technical vulnerabilities directly connect to compliance obligations. ISO/IEC 27001 Annex A.12.6 emphasises vulnerability management, while GDPR Article 32 highlights secure data processing (ISO, 2022; European Union, 2016). NIST SP 800-53 controls such as SI-2 (Flaw Remediation) and AC-2 (Account Management) provided guidance on structured remediation (NIST, 2020). PCI DSS reinforced the need for continuous patching and monitoring to protect cardholder data (PCI SSC, 2022). Overall, the activity showed that audits must not stop at technical fixes, but be interpreted within governance and regulatory frameworks.

Application:

In FinTech, where sensitive financial data is at stake, these insights are highly relevant. Regulators expect evidence that vulnerabilities are addressed and compliance demonstrated. Linking cloud controls to ISO 27001, NIST, PCI DSS, and GDPR provides assurance of due diligence. Similarly, the disaster recovery design exercise reinforced how RPO and RTO targets map to ISO 22301, ensuring both resilience and compliance (ISO, 2019). This mapping directly informs operational practices in my role, where audits, compliance reviews, and regulator expectations converge.

Forward Planning:

I plan to deepen my knowledge of cloud compliance by experimenting with automated compliance monitoring tools, such as AWS Config and Azure Policy, to map controls continuously against ISO 27001 and GDPR. Developing stronger skills in OpenVAS and other vulnerability management tools will allow me to connect technical findings with compliance requirements more effectively. This will prepare me for governance-driven roles where bridging technical risk and regulatory obligations is critical.

Reflective Thinking:

Summary:

This week focused on disaster recovery (DR) and business continuity, highlighting how cloud-based strategies can achieve resilience objectives. The readings (Carson and Rodrick, 2024; Zheng et al., 2024) emphasised the need to define Recovery Time Objectives (RTO) and Recovery Point Objectives (RPO), then design DR solutions that balance cost, performance, and compliance. The coursework activity reinforced this by simulating recovery on OpenStack using open-source tools such as Bacula or Duplicity.

Reflection:

I reflected on how DR is not only a technical requirement but also a governance obligation. ISO 22301 provides a framework for business continuity management, while ISO/IEC 27001 and GDPR require demonstrable resilience and data protection (ISO, 2019; ISO, 2022; European Union, 2016). In practice, I recognised that achieving a low RTO/RPO involves trade-offs: tighter objectives improve resilience but increase cost and complexity. The OpenStack exercise showed how open-source tools can provide flexibility, but they also demand strong operational discipline to avoid misconfiguration risks.

Application:

In the FinTech sector, regulators such as the FCA require firms to demonstrate robust DR and continuity strategies. For example, ensuring payment processing systems can recover within 1 hour (RTO) and limiting data loss to minutes (RPO) is vital to customer trust and compliance. Using multi-cloud patterns, such as active-active replication between AWS and Azure, could enhance resilience while mitigating vendor lock-in. The activity also showed that DR must integrate with broader compliance frameworks, linking directly to audits and governance reporting.

Forward Planning:

I plan to expand my knowledge of DRaaS (Disaster Recovery as a Service) providers such as Zerto and Veeam, while also exploring how policy-as-code can embed RTO/RPO objectives into infrastructure-as-code pipelines. This will prepare me to design DR strategies that are not only technically effective but also compliant, auditable, and aligned with ISO 22301 standards.

Reflection:

Summary:

This week focused on hybrid cloud solutions and practical migration exercises. The lecturecast on Hybrid Cloud highlighted design patterns where OpenStack integrates with public clouds for elasticity and compliance (Maurer, 2021). The formative coursework involved migrating a MySQL database to OpenStack using Percona XtraBackup. Attempting this on Windows exposed constraints with tooling support, requiring workarounds such as staging backups through Linux-based environments to achieve minimal downtime. The readings further explored cloud security through deep learning (Zhou et al., 2023) and the strategic trade-offs between cloud-agnostic and vendor-locked DevOps approaches (Dunbar, 2024).

Reflection:

I reflected on how hybrid cloud design requires balancing control and flexibility. Maurer (2021) emphasised governance and workload placement, while the migration exercise reinforced that technical constraints—like limited Windows support for Percona tools—can shape architecture choices. The workaround demonstrated resilience but also highlighted risks of misconfiguration. The article on machine learning in cloud security showed how predictive models can strengthen proactive defence, while the vendor lock-in piece illustrated that even cloud-agnostic aspirations come with trade-offs, often requiring more expertise and cost (Dunbar, 2024). This reminded me that migrations and hybrid strategies must align with both compliance and operational realities.

Application:

In financial services, migrating core databases demands both data consistency and near-zero downtime. Using tools like Percona XtraBackup is relevant, but production environments would require clustering, replication, and automated failover. Hybrid solutions also provide opportunities to isolate sensitive workloads on OpenStack while leveraging cloud elasticity for analytics. AI-driven monitoring, as described by Zhou et al. (2023), could further secure these deployments. At the same time, vendor-locked tooling such as Azure DevOps may accelerate pipelines but needs conscious governance to prevent strategic dependency.

Forward Planning:

I plan to test database migrations using Linux-native environments to minimise workarounds and experiment with hybrid models that blend OpenStack with AWS or Azure. I also intend to explore AIOps platforms for predictive security and evaluate cloud-agnostic orchestration tools like Kubernetes against vendor-locked CI/CD to balance flexibility and integration. Building these skills will prepare me for architecting resilient, compliant, and strategically balanced hybrid environments.
