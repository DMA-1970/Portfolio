Title: Case Study on Privacy

Please read the case below and answer the questions. You need to determine the ethical issues involved with this case.

The Case

Ricardo works for the records department of his local government as a computer records clerk, where he has access to files of property tax records. For a scientific study, a researcher, Beth, has been granted access to the numerical portion "but not the corresponding names" of some records.

Beth finds some information that she would like to use, but she needs the names and addresses corresponding with certain properties. Beth asks Ricardo to retrieve these names and addresses, so she can contact these people for more information and for permission to do further study.

Now consider, what are the ethical issues involved in deciding which of these options to pursue?

  • If Ricardo is not responsible for determining allowable access, should he release the names and addresses?
  • Suppose Ricardo were responsible for determining allowable access to the files. What ethical issues would be involved in his deciding whether to grant access to Beth?
  • Should Beth be allowed to contact the individuals involved? That is, should the Records department release individuals' names to a researcher? What are the ethical issues for the Records department to consider?
  • Suppose Beth contacts the individuals to ask their permission, and one-third of them respond giving permission, one-third respond denying permission, and one-third do not respond. Beth claims that at least one-half of the individuals are needed to make a valid study. What options are available to Beth?

Introduction

This document provides ten critiques of the initial answers to the Ricardo‚ÄìBeth case and a refined, academically framed set of responses to each question. It aligns with UK GDPR, the Data Protection Act 2018, and professional ethics (ACM & BCS), with attention to research governance and methodological integrity.

Ten Critiques and Corrections

1. Lawful basis clarity: For a local authority, the usual basis is Public Task (UK GDPR Art. 6(1)(e)); Legitimate Interests is generally inapplicable when performing public functions.

2. Controller vs processor roles: Clarify that the Records Department is the controller; Beth (or her institution) would be an independent controller or a processor only if a contract meeting Art. 28 exists.

3. Research provisions: Recognise Article 89 safeguards (e.g., pseudonymisation, access controls) and the limits on decisions about individuals based solely on research data.

4. Anonymisation vs pseudonymisation: 'Numerical-only' fields may still be re-identifiable in small-area datasets; treat as pseudonymised unless demonstrably anonymised.

5. DPIA and governance: Re-identification risk and contact activity suggest a DPIA, Data Sharing Agreement (DSA), ethics approval, and an audit trail before any disclosure.

6. Purpose limitation and compatibility: Data collected for taxation needs compatibility assessment (Art. 6(4)) before reuse for research contact.

7. Data minimisation and proportionality: Names/addresses should remain with the controller wherever possible; prefer a gatekeeper contact model.

8. Transparency and fair processing: Ensure appropriate Art. 13/14 notices or public notices are in place before contact; avoid surprise uses.

9. Methodological integrity: Non-response bias must be explicitly managed (e.g., power analysis, weighting, design adjustments) rather than lowering consent standards.

10. Data subject rights and retention: Plan for rights requests (access, objection, erasure where applicable) and define minimal retention consistent with research needs.

Refined Answers to Individual Questions

1. If Ricardo is not responsible for determining allowable access, should he release the names and addresses?

No. Disclosing personally identifiable information (PII) without a lawful basis or delegated authority would breach UK GDPR and the Data Protection Act 2018. It would also violate professional duties of confidentiality and authorised access (ACM 2018; BCS 2023). Ricardo should refuse and escalate to the Data Protection Officer (DPO) or controller representative.

2. Suppose Ricardo were responsible for determining allowable access to the files. What ethical issues would be involved in his deciding whether to grant access to Beth?

He must balance research value against privacy rights and legal constraints. Prior to any disclosure, he should require a Data Sharing Agreement, DPIA, ethics approval, and clear specification of roles (controller/processor). He should apply Public Task (Art. 6(1)(e)) only if purpose compatibility (Art. 6(4)) and Article 89 safeguards are satisfied, and ensure strict data minimisation and security.

3. Should Beth be allowed to contact the individuals involved? Should the Records Department release individuals' names to a researcher?

Not directly. A gatekeeper model is preferable: the Records Department (as controller) contacts individuals on Beth's behalf to invite consent. Releasing names/addresses to Beth would exceed data minimisation without a clear lawful basis and would undermine confidentiality.

4. What are the ethical issues for the Records Department to consider?

Lawfulness and purpose limitation; transparency (appropriate notices); safeguards (pseudonymisation, access controls); accountability (audit trails, staff training, DPO consultation); and data subject rights and retention. Failure risks legal non-compliance and loss of public trust.

5. If one-third consent, one-third refuse, and one-third do not respond‚Äîbut at least half are needed for validity‚Äîwhat options are available to Beth?

She cannot treat non-response as consent. Options: proceed with consenting data and manage bias (power analysis, weighting); request controller-led reminders; revise design or expand recruitment following ethics approval; or use anonymised/aggregated outputs generated by the controller.
