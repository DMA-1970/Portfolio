1. Introduction

Online surveys are widely used to gather opinions, personalise services, and support research. However, several high-profile incidents demonstrate how seemingly harmless survey tools can be misused for large-scale data harvesting, profiling, and manipulation. This entry examines how Cambridge Analytica used Facebook surveys to obtain and exploit user data, identifies further examples of inappropriate survey use, and evaluates the ethical, social, legal, and professional implications of these practices.

2. Cambridge Analytica: How the Survey Led to Data Misuse

2.1 Mechanism of the Data Harvesting

In 2013, a Facebook app called thisisyourdigitallife was presented as a harmless personality quiz for academic research. Approximately 270,000 users installed the app and completed its survey. At the time, Facebook's API allowed apps to access not only the data of survey participants but also certain data belonging to all of their Facebook friends.

This loophole enabled the app's developers to harvest data from up to 87 million Facebook users, most of whom had never directly consented. The collected data included public profiles, likes, demographic information, and elements of the users' social graph.

2.2 Why the Data Was Used

Although framed as academic research, the data was transferred to Cambridge Analytica. Using psychographic modelling based on OCEAN personality traits, the company developed behavioural profiles and deployed them for political micro-targeting during major campaigns such as the 2016 US presidential election (Aagaard, 2023; Carrella et al., 2025).

The goal was to influence behaviour by identifying individuals' psychological vulnerabilities and targeting them with highly personalised political messages (Bakir, 2020; Hu, 2020).

3. Additional Examples of Inappropriate Survey Use

3.1 The myPersonality Facebook App

The myPersonality survey app, created by Cambridge University researchers, collected detailed psychometric profiles from millions of users. The data was then shared, often insecurely, with external researchers and industry partners. Poor access controls enabled unauthorised parties to download sensitive datasets, compromising the privacy of over 4 million users. Facebook subsequently banned the app for refusing an audit and for inadequate data protection practices.

3.2 Wider Abuse: Mass Suspension of Quiz Apps

Following the Cambridge Analytica revelations, Facebook suspended around 200 survey and quiz apps as part of an internal investigation. Many had been using "fun" quizzes to secretly collect extensive personal data. This demonstrated that inappropriate survey use was systemic, not exceptional, and that many apps exploited platform weaknesses to over-collect data (van der Schyff, Flowerday and Furnell, 2020).

4. Ethical Implications

Survey misuse raises significant ethical concerns:

  • Lack of informed consent: Users were led to believe they were taking part in harmless research or entertainment. Most were unaware their data ‚Äì and their friends' data ‚Äì would be used for political profiling (Breuer, Turbah and Rinke, 2021).
  • Deception and purpose misrepresentation: The stated purpose of the surveys did not match the actual intent, violating principles of honesty, autonomy, and respect for persons (Singh et al., 2022; Cilliers and Viljoen, 2020).
  • Manipulation and exploitation: Psychographic targeting exploited individuals' psychological traits, raising questions about undue influence and manipulation.
  • Violation of privacy and dignity: Harvesting data from non-participants (friends) breached core ethical principles of digital rights and user agency (Hanlon, 2023).

5. Social Implications

  • Erosion of trust: Public trust in social media platforms and digital services declined sharply following the scandal. Many users became more sceptical about online data collection (Ho et al., 2023; Aagaard, 2023).
  • Impact on democracy: Micro-targeted political adverts undermined transparency in democratic processes by delivering messages that were not publicly visible or subject to scrutiny (Carrella et al., 2025).
  • Digital inequality: People with lower digital literacy were more likely to engage with these surveys without understanding their implications, contributing to unequal exposure.
  • Normalisation of surveillance: The incidents reinforced a culture where constant data extraction is viewed as unavoidable.

6. Legal Implications

  • Data protection violations: Regulators found that Facebook failed to protect user data and enforce its terms. Fines included ¬£500,000 from the UK ICO and a $5 billion penalty from the US FTC (Huang, 2024).
  • GDPR breaches: The practices contravened GDPR principles, including lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, and data minimisation.
  • Accountability and platform obligations: The incident prompted global scrutiny of tech companies and strengthened the argument for tougher regulation of data-driven platforms (van der Schyff, Flowerday and Furnell, 2020).
  • Class-action settlements: Meta later paid a $725 million settlement for improperly sharing user data.

7. Professional Implications

Standards such as the ACM Code of Ethics and BCS Code of Conduct emphasise responsibilities to protect user privacy, avoid harm, and ensure transparency.

Professionally, the case highlights failures to:

  • Apply responsible data governance and minimise harm.
  • Communicate data usage clearly.
  • Implement robust data access controls.
  • Ensure that research and development activities align with professional codes and societal expectations (Cilliers and Viljoen, 2020; Breuer, Turbah and Rinke, 2021).

For computing professionals, the events stress the need for stronger ethical awareness, transparency, and accountability in system design and data collection practices.

8. Conclusion

These cases demonstrate that survey tools‚Äîeven those appearing harmless‚Äîcan be exploited for large-scale data harvesting and behavioural manipulation. The Cambridge Analytica incident, the myPersonality app, and the mass suspension of similar tools collectively highlight the importance of ethical design, informed consent, and strict data governance. From ethical, social, legal, and professional standpoints, the misuse of surveys undermines public trust, violates privacy, threatens democratic processes, and breaches the duties expected of computing professionals.

Ethical and transparent handling of survey data is essential for maintaining public trust and upholding professional standards in the digital age.
