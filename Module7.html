Key themes included descriptive visualisation, qualitative coding, dashboard use, and the ethical risks of misleading graphical presentation. Berenson et al. (2015) emphasise that charts shape interpretation as much as they report results, while industry sources highlight the growing reliance on dashboards to convert data into decisions. The seminar reinforced how inappropriate chart choices, distorted scales, and selective emphasis can obscure meaning.

Reflection: A central learning point was that data presentation is not neutral. Even statistically sound analyses can mislead if visual design exaggerates differences or removes context. Seminar examples of conflicting scientific headlines illustrated how graphical and narrative framing can bias interpretation. The charts worksheet demonstrated this practically: percentage frequency bar charts and relative frequency histograms enabled meaningful comparisons, but outcomes remained sensitive to class intervals, bin widths, and scaling choices. For example, Diet A appeared more consistent than Diet B, yet this conclusion depends on how distributions are constructed and displayed. Qualitative analysis further highlighted subjectivity. Coding decisions and thematic aggregation introduce interpretive bias, reinforcing the need for reflexivity and transparency when presenting qualitative findings.

Application: Academically, this unit informs how I will justify both analytical methods and visual choices in my research proposal and dissertation. The worksheet exercises showed how well-structured visualisations support clarity when appropriately matched to the data and research question. Professionally, these lessons align closely with enterprise reporting and governance. Dashboards frequently inform executive and regulatory decisions, making accuracy and proportionality essential. The Microsoft (2023) reading reinforces that effective dashboards prioritise interpretability over visual complexity.

Forward Planning: I will apply greater critical scrutiny to both creating and interpreting visualisations, explicitly justifying design choices and acknowledging limitations. This unit also prepares me for Unit 9 by highlighting that visual validity‚Äîfaithfully representing data‚Äîis integral to overall research validity and ethical analysis.

Reflective Thinking:

Summary: This unit focused on validity and generalisability as core measures of research quality, linking research design decisions to the credibility of findings. The Lecturecast clarified the distinction between internal validity (accuracy within the study) and external validity (the extent findings can be generalised beyond the study context). Saunders et al. (2023) emphasise that validity is strengthened through careful instrument design, clear coverage of investigative questions, and rigorous reliability checks, while Schneider (2018) highlights that qualitative approaches using unconventional sources require credibility through contextual depth and transparent interpretation.

Reflection: I found the Lecturecast particularly enlightening because it framed validity as a practical judgement rather than a theoretical label. The week also challenged the assumption that generalisation is primarily statistical; qualitative evidence can be transferable when it is well-contextualised and analytically coherent. I found the Power BI exercise particularly interesting, as it demonstrated how visual design choices (filters, aggregation, and chart selection) can either support valid interpretation or unintentionally distort meaning. This reinforced that "validity" also applies to how results are communicated, not only how data is collected.

Application: Academically, this aligns with Module 7 learning outcomes by strengthening my ability to justify methodological fit, evaluate rigour, and explain limitations affecting generalisability. Professionally, the link between validity and visualisation is directly relevant to governance reporting: dashboards often inform executive and regulatory decisions, so accuracy and proportionality are essential. The collaborative discussion on accuracy of information reinforced the ethical responsibility to avoid misleading representation, even when underlying data is correct.

Forward Planning: I will apply clearer validity checks in future work by explicitly documenting assumptions, bias risks, and the intended scope of generalisation. I will also develop Power BI skills further, focusing on reproducible dashboard logic and transparent metrics. These steps support my final research project direction by strengthening my capacity to justify research design choices and ensure that analytical outputs and visualisations faithfully represent findings in enterprise and governance contexts.
