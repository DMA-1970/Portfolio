Reflective Thinking:

Summary: Unit 2 advanced the concepts introduced in Unit 1 by transforming ethical and philosophical foundations into practical research design. Dr Navarra's (2025) lecture connected the scientific method with professional accountability, showing how rigorous inquiry begins with a clear problem statement, purposeful literature review, and transparent methodology. Dawson (2015) emphasised aligning research questions with measurable objectives, while the Belmont (2018) and Menlo (Finn & Shilton, 2023) Reports highlighted ethical obligations in computing research, especially in AI-driven contexts.

Reflection: Reflecting on Units 1 and 2 together, I realised that ethics and methodology are inseparable in computing research. The ACM and BCS codes reinforced that integrity, transparency, and avoidance of harm must underpin every research stage. Correa et al. (2023) and Fjeld et al. (2020) broadened my understanding of global AI governance frameworks, while Dr Navarra's discussion on reasoning helped me balance deductive rigour with inductive openness. From a CIO and governance standpoint, I recognised that ethical oversight and balanced stakeholder influence are vital for ensuring that research outcomes align with both organisational integrity and societal trust.

Application: I employed a combination of qualitative and interpretivist approaches to identify patterns within academic sources and professional codes, guided by Dawson's (2015) structured methodology. To ensure clarity and consistency, I adopted the PEER framework (Point, Evidence, Explanation, Relevance) when drafting and reviewing sections of my literature review. This method allowed me to establish a clear argument (Point), support it with credible academic or professional sources (Evidence), interpret its significance (Explanation), and link it back to the research objectives or ethical principles (Relevance). Using PEER enhanced my critical writing and ensured that each paragraph was traceable to a specific research purpose.

I also explored digital research tools such as Zotero for citation management and Grammarly for ethical proofreading, ensuring originality and academic integrity. By adhering to Cite Them Right Harvard referencing standards, I maintained compliance with University of Essex academic conduct guidelines. This process mirrors enterprise-level documentation and auditability ‚Äî key for governance transparency and ethical assurance in both academia and corporate research environments.

Forward Planning: Building on Units 1‚Äì2, I intend to refine my literature review and proposal by integrating PEER methodology across all analytical sections, ensuring evidence-based reasoning throughout. Using Healey et al. (2020) and Boza (2022), I will continue improving the scholarly flow while embedding ethical validation steps inspired by the Menlo and Belmont frameworks. I plan to extend this structured approach into future modules, treating research ethics as a living governance process ‚Äî where clarity, reproducibility, and transparency drive both academic and professional excellence.
