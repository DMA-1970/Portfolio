Weekly Reflection 3

This week explored how research design choices influence validity, reliability, and credibility in computing research. Dawson (2015) and Saunders et al. (2012) showed how exploratory and conclusive aims drive the selection of qualitative, quantitative, or mixed methods. Comparing Alasmary et al.'s (2021) experimental phishing study with Mittelstadt's (2019) interpretive AI ethics analysis demonstrated that statistical rigour and contextual insight are complementary. This mirrors my professional practice, where metrics-led security reporting must align with governance frameworks such as ISO 27001 and GDPR. I will apply this learning by developing a small mixed-method pilot study for my MSc project.

Weekly Reflection 10

This unit reinforced that effective research writing is about structured argument, not volume. Producing the 15-minute proposal forced prioritisation of contribution, gap, method, evaluation, and ethics. I felt a quiet anxiety about accuracy and academic credibility, driving multiple revisions. The constraint sharpened my judgement, showing that concise delivery can be more rigorous when logic is auditable (Booth, Colomb and Williams, 2016). I applied this by consolidating regulatory references and simplifying timelines into phases. Going forward, I will draft then reduce, explicitly evidencing claims and limitations.
